Protocols

Culture

Aim

Maintain viable Pseudomonas putida F1 cultures through routine passaging to support metabolic engineering, biodegradation studies, or synthetic biology experiments while preserving genetic stability. [ATCC]

Materials Required

  • Sterile Luria–Bertani (LB) broth
  • Sterile 5–10 mL culture tubes or flasks
  • Sterile pipettes (Pasteur or 1 mL) with tips
  • Shaking incubator (30°C, 200–250 rpm)
  • Spectrophotometer (for OD600 measurement)
  • Glycerol stock (−80°C) or fresh agar plate (≤ 4 days old) of Pseudomonas putida F1

From Frozen Stock (Initial Recovery)

  1. Open the vial according to the enclosed instructions.
  2. Using a single tube of #3 broth (5–6 mL), withdraw approximately 0.5–1.0 mL with a Pasteur or 1.0 mL pipette and use it to rehydrate the pellet.
  3. Aseptically transfer this aliquot back into the broth tube and mix well.
  4. Use several drops of the suspension to inoculate a #3 agar slant and/or plate.
  5. Incubate the tubes and plates at 30°C for 24–48 hours.

Routine Subculture

  • From an overnight culture (OD600 ≈ 1–2), transfer 50–100 µL (1–5% inoculum) into 5 mL of fresh LB broth.
  • Incubate at 30°C and 225 rpm until OD600 reaches 0.5–1.0 (exponential phase), or incubate overnight to reach stationary phase.
  • Repeat subculturing every 24–48 hours; streak onto plates if single colonies are required.
  • For long-term storage, mix stationary-phase culture 1:1 with 30% sterile glycerol and freeze at −80°C.

Note

Use aseptic technique at all times. Optimal growth conditions are 25–30°C under aerobic conditions. Avoid more than four sequential passages from the original stock without replating.

Bacterial Confirmatory Tests

1) Gram Staining

Aim

To determine the Gram reaction and classify Pseudomonas putida as Gram-positive or Gram-negative based on cell wall properties.

Procedure

  1. Sample Preparation:
    Using an inoculating loop, aseptically obtain a small amount of Pseudomonas putida from the broth culture and spread the sample in a thin, even smear on a clean glass slide. Allow the slide to air-dry.
  2. Heat Fixing:
    Once the smear is dry, pass the slide through the flame of a Bunsen burner 2–3 times, smear-side up, to heat-fix the cells to the slide. Avoid overheating to prevent distortion of cell structure.
  3. Primary Staining:
    Flood the heat-fixed smear with crystal violet stain and allow it to sit for 1 minute. Rinse gently with distilled water until excess stain is removed.
  4. Mordant Application:
    Add Gram’s iodine solution to cover the smear completely and let it sit for 1 minute. Rinse gently with distilled water.
  5. Decolourisation:
    Decolourise by adding 95% ethanol dropwise over the smear for 10–20 seconds, or until the runoff is clear. Immediately rinse with distilled water to stop the process.
  6. Counterstaining:
    Flood the smear with safranin stain and allow it to sit for 30 seconds. Rinse gently with distilled water and allow the slide to air-dry completely.
  7. Microscopy:
    Once dry, add a drop of immersion oil to the smear and observe under the oil-immersion (100×) objective of a microscope.

2) Indole Test

Aim

To differentiate members of the Enterobacteriaceae family.

Procedure

  1. Sample Preparation:
    Pipette a drop of Pseudomonas putida broth culture onto a clean filter paper.
  2. Addition of Kovac’s Reagent:
    Add 1–2 drops of Kovac’s reagent directly onto the same spot on the filter paper where the broth was applied.
  3. Observation:
    Observe the colour change on the filter paper:
    • Red or pink colour indicates a positive indole test.
    • No colour change or yellow colour indicates a negative indole test.

3) Methyl Red (MR) Test

Aim

To determine whether Pseudomonas putida ferments glucose to produce stable acidic end products.

Procedure

  1. Inoculation:
    Inoculate MR–VP broth with a loopful of Pseudomonas putida from a fresh culture. Incubate at 32°C for 48 hours.
  2. Addition of Methyl Red:
    After incubation, transfer 1 mL of culture into a sterile test tube and add 5 drops of Methyl Red indicator.
  3. Observation:
    Observe the colour immediately:
    • Red colour indicates a positive MR test.
    • Yellow colour or no colour change indicates a negative MR test.

4) Voges–Proskauer (VP) Test

Aim

To determine whether Pseudomonas putida ferments glucose to produce neutral end products.

Procedure

  1. Inoculation:
    Use the same MR–VP broth culture from the MR test. Transfer 1 mL of the Pseudomonas putida culture into a clean test tube.
  2. Addition of Barritt’s Reagents:
    Add 15 drops of Barritt’s A (α-naphthol), followed by 5 drops of Barritt’s B (potassium hydroxide). Gently shake to mix.
  3. Observation:
    Allow the tube to stand for 15–30 minutes:
    • Reddish colour at the surface indicates a positive VP test.
    • No colour change or copper colour indicates a negative VP test.

Growth Curve

Six-hour Growth Curve

Aim: To analyze the growth pattern and calculate the generation time of Pseudomonas putida under specific conditions.

Procedure:

  1. Preparation of Overnight Culture:
    Inoculate 5–10 mL of LB medium with Pseudomonas putida and incubate at 37°C with shaking (180–200 rpm) for 12 hours to reach the stationary phase.
  2. Subculturing into Fresh LB Broth (Test Tube):
    Prepare a fresh test tube containing 5 mL of LB broth. Transfer 100 μL of the 12-hour culture into the tube and incubate until the culture reaches an OD600 of 0.7.
  3. Inoculation into Flask:
    Prepare flasks containing 25 mL of fresh LB broth. Perform a 1:500 dilution by adding 50 μL of the OD600 0.7 culture into each flask.
  4. Incubation:
    Place the flasks in a shaking incubator at 37°C with shaking at 200–250 rpm for aeration. Record the initial OD600 value (time 0) immediately after inoculation.
  5. Measurement of OD600:
    At regular intervals (every 30 minutes), withdraw 1 mL of culture from the flask and measure OD600 using a spectrophotometer. Record all values.
  6. Plotting the Growth Curve:
    Continue measurements until the culture reaches the stationary phase (typically 12–18 hours). Plot OD600 against time to visualize the lag, log (exponential), stationary, and death phases.

24-hour Growth Curve

Procedure:

  1. Preparation of Overnight Culture:
    Inoculate 5–10 mL of LB medium with Pseudomonas putida and incubate at 37°C with shaking (180–200 rpm) for 12 hours to reach the stationary phase.
  2. Subculturing into Fresh LB Broth (Test Tube):
    Prepare a fresh test tube containing 5 mL of LB broth. Transfer 100 μL of the 12-hour culture into the tube and incubate until the culture reaches an OD600 of 0.7.
  3. Inoculation into Flask:
    Prepare flasks containing 25 mL of fresh LB broth. Perform a 1:500 dilution by adding 50 μL of the OD600 0.7 culture into each flask.
  4. Incubation:
    Incubate the flasks at 37°C with shaking at 200–250 rpm. Record the initial OD600 reading at time 0 after inoculation.
  5. Measurement of OD600:
    At regular intervals (every 1 hour), withdraw 1 mL of culture from the flask and measure OD600 using a spectrophotometer. Record all readings. OD600 values from 0–12 hours were recorded at 2-hour intervals from two flasks, while 12–24 hour readings were recorded from flasks prepared on the previous day.
  6. Plotting the Growth Curve:
    Plot OD600 values against time to generate the growth curve, illustrating the lag, log (exponential), stationary, and death phases.

Knock-out protocol

pT18mobsacB Plasmid Digestion

Selection of Restriction Sites

  • Open the pT18mobsacB plasmid map in SnapGene (or similar DNA mapping software) and locate the multiple cloning site (MCS), which contains several unique restriction sites.
  • For efficient downstream ligation or assembly, linearise the plasmid using double digestion with two restriction enzymes.
  • In pT18mobsacB, it is recommended to avoid the XbaI restriction site. Suitable sites include XmaI and PstI.
  • Select two restriction sites separated by approximately 20 base pairs on the plasmid map to minimise self-ligation and incomplete digestion.
  • Other enzyme pairs may be used if they uniquely cut outside essential regions, but XmaI/PstI is considered optimal.

Plasmid Digestion

  • Digest the pT18mobsacB plasmid with the selected restriction enzymes.
  • Set up a 50 μL reaction on ice:
  • 10× FastDigest Buffer: 5 μL
  • XmaI FastDigest enzyme: 2.5 μL
  • PstI FastDigest enzyme: 2.5 μL
  • pT18mobsacB plasmid DNA: 2.5 μg
  • Nuclease-free water: to 50 μL

Mix gently, briefly centrifuge or flick the tube, and incubate at 37°C for 20 minutes. Heat-inactivate enzymes at 98°C for 5 minutes, then cool to 4°C.

Recovery of the Digested Plasmid

  • If digestion is clean, purify the linearised plasmid directly using a silica column-based DNA purification kit.
  • Use the FastPure Gel DNA Extraction Mini Kit (Vazyme, DC301) following the manufacturer’s instructions.
  • Bind DNA to the column, wash to remove enzymes and small fragments, and elute in water or elution buffer.
  • Measure DNA concentration and purity.

A successful outcome is high-purity linearised pT18mobsacB ready for ligation.

PCR Amplification of UHA and DHA

  • Perform high-fidelity PCR to amplify UHA and DHA from genomic DNA using designed primers.
  • Prepare two 50 μL PCR reactions with the following components:
  • 2× Phanta Max Master Mix (Dye Plus): 25 μL
  • Forward primer: 2 μL
  • Reverse primer: 2 μL
  • Template genomic DNA: 1.5 μL
  • Nuclease-free ddH2O: to 50 μL

PCR cycling conditions:

  • Initial denaturation: 95°C for 3 min
  • Denaturation: 95°C for 15 s
  • Annealing: 56–72°C for 15 s
  • Extension: 72°C for 60–90 s
  • 25–35 cycles (30 cycles recommended)
  • Final extension: 72°C for 5 min
  • Hold: 4°C

Verify amplification by agarose gel electrophoresis (1% gel). Expected sizes: UHA 0.8–1 kb; DHA 0.5–0.8 kb.

Plasmid Assembly (Ligation)

  • Use the ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme, C112).
  • Set up a 10 μL reaction on ice:
  • 5× ClonExpress II Buffer: 2 μL
  • Exnase II enzyme mix: 1 μL
  • Linearised pT18mobsacB vector: 62 ng
  • Purified UHA fragment: 20 ng
  • Purified DHA fragment: 16 ng
  • DEPC-treated water: to 10 μL

Incubate at 37°C for 30 minutes, then place on ice or at 4°C. The resulting plasmid is pT18-UHA-DHA.

Transformation into E. coli DH5α by Heat Shock

  • Mix 5–10 μL of assembly reaction with 20–50 μL chemically competent E. coli DH5α cells.
  • Incubate on ice for 30 minutes, heat-shock at 42°C for 45–90 seconds, then return to ice for 2 minutes.
  • Add 200 μL LB broth (no antibiotic) and incubate at 37°C with shaking for 1–1.5 hours.
  • Plate on LB agar containing tetracycline (20 μg/mL) and incubate overnight.

Conjugative Transformation

  • Plate transformed E. coli S17 with Pseudomonas putida F1 together on LB agar.
  • Transfer colonies to LB containing tetracycline and nalidixic acid to select for Pseudomonas transconjugants.

Sucrose Counter-selection

  • Inoculate single-crossover integrants into LB without antibiotics and incubate overnight at 30°C.
  • Plate diluted cultures onto LB agar containing 20% sucrose without tetracycline.
  • Screen sucrose-resistant colonies for tetracycline sensitivity.

Screening and Sequencing of Knockout Mutants

  • Perform colony PCR using outer homologous arm primers (UHA-F and DHA-R).
  • Knockout mutants yield a shorter PCR product compared to wild type.
  • Confirm deletion using gene-specific primers (no amplification expected in knockouts).
  • Analyse PCR products by agarose gel electrophoresis with wild-type controls.

Minimum Inhibitory Concentration

Aim

The aim of this assay was to determine the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) of p-cuminaldehyde against Pseudomonas putida F1 using a broth microdilution method followed by colony-forming unit (CFU) confirmation.

Materials

  • Pseudomonas putida F1 culture
  • M9 minimal medium supplemented with sodium lactate
  • Cuminaldehyde stock solution (prepared in DMSO or ethanol)
  • 96-well sterile microtiter plates with sealing film
  • Sterile micropipettes and pipette tips
  • Spectrophotometer or plate reader (OD600)
  • LB agar plates for CFU determination
  • Incubator shaker set at 30 °C

Preparation of Inoculum

P. putida F1 was revived on LB agar plates and incubated at 30 °C for 24 hours.

A single colony was inoculated into LB broth and incubated overnight at 30 °C with shaking. Cells were harvested by centrifugation and washed twice with sterile M9 salts to remove residual LB medium.

Preparation of Cuminaldehyde Dilutions

Serial dilutions of cuminaldehyde were prepared in M9 minimal medium over a concentration range of 0–4 mM.

The solvent concentration (DMSO or ethanol) was kept constant across all wells, including growth and sterility controls.

Plate Setup

180 µL of inoculated M9 medium was dispensed into each well of a sterile 96-well microtiter plate. 20 µL of the appropriate cuminaldehyde dilution was added to each well to achieve the desired final concentrations.

Growth controls (medium with inoculum but no cuminaldehyde) and sterility controls (medium only) were included in each assay.

Incubation

The microtiter plate was sealed to minimize volatilization and incubated at 30 °C for 24–48 hours with gentle shaking.

MIC Determination

Following incubation, bacterial growth was assessed by measuring optical density at OD600. The MIC was defined as the lowest concentration of cuminaldehyde that resulted in no visible growth compared to the growth control.

CFU Confirmation

To distinguish between bacteriostatic and bactericidal effects, aliquots from the MIC well and wells containing concentrations above the MIC were plated onto LB agar.

Plates were incubated at 30 °C for 24 hours and colonies were counted. The absence of colony formation indicated bactericidal activity, whereas the presence of colonies indicated a bacteriostatic effect.

Extraction

Cell Cultivation

Pseudomonas putida f1 was cultured overnight in M9 minimal media at 30 °C with shaking at 220 rpm. The medium was supplemented with 20 mM sodium lactate as an additional energy source.

Cell Immobilization Using Triblock Polymer

The cultured cells were transferred to a shake flask and immobilized using the triblock polymer PCl21-PEG45-PCl21 through the formation of an inverse multi-emulsion system.

The suitability of the polymer for forming an inverse multi-emulsion was evaluated by calculating its hydrophilic–lipophilic balance (HLB) value. The HLB values determined from NMR and GPC data were 5.3 and 5.8, respectively (data from literature), confirming the polymer’s ability to form a stable inverse multi-emulsion system.

Formation of the Inverse Multi-Emulsion

To prepare the organic phase, the polymer was dissolved in toluene at a concentration of 20 mg/mL. The aqueous phase consisted of cells suspended in media containing sodium lactate at a concentration of 10 mg/mL.

The emulsion was formed by adding the organic phase to the aqueous phase followed by gentle hand shaking. A water-to-oil ratio of 6:4 was maintained throughout the process.

Capsule Formation and Stabilization

This process resulted in the formation of capsules containing culture broth with cells confined within the liquid aqueous core, surrounded by an outer organic phase consisting of toluene. A clear phase distinction between the aqueous and organic layers was observed.

The system was incubated for 30 minutes to allow stable encapsulation and equilibrium to be established (Zhao et al., 2020).

Assessment of Cell Viability by Plating

To compare the viability of encapsulated cells with native cells, samples from both systems were dispersed in phosphate buffer to maintain stability. The samples were adjusted to the same optical density (OD600), and 50 µL of each was plated on nutrient agar.

Plates were incubated at 37 °C for 12 hours prior to analysis (Zhao et al., 2020).

Fluorescence-Based Viability Staining

Cell viability was further assessed using fluorescent staining, where viable cells were stained green with SYTO 9 and non-viable cells were stained red using propidium iodide (PI).

Between 100–500 µL of the emulsion was sampled at 0.5 h, 1 h, and 3 h. Excess 10× PBS buffer (1–5 mL) was added to preserve cell viability.

The samples were gently mixed and incubated for 5–10 minutes to break the aqueous droplets and release the cells into the buffer. Centrifugation was performed at 2000 rpm for 5 minutes at room temperature to sediment the cells, after which the top oil layer was removed.

The cell pellet was resuspended in 500 µL PBS. 3 µM SYTO 9 and 30 µM PI were added, and the cells were incubated in the dark for 15 minutes. The cells were washed once with PBS prior to imaging (Zhao et al., 2020).

Substrate Addition and Aldehyde Production

The concentration of p-cymene required was calculated based on the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC). p-cymene, the substrate required to trigger the catabolic pathway leading to cuminaldehyde accumulation, was added to toluene to form a homogeneous mixture.

p-cymene diffused through the polymer matrix into the cells, where it entered the p-cymene catabolic pathway, resulting in the production of p-cuminaldehyde. Accumulation occurred due to the knockout of the gene responsible for converting p-cuminaldehyde to p-cumate.

Product Diffusion and Phase Partitioning

Owing to its toxicity, p-cuminaldehyde diffused out of the cell membrane (Simmonds & Robinson, 1998), passed through the polymer matrix, and partitioned into the organic solvent. At this stage, the organic phase consisted of toluene, p-cymene, and p-cuminaldehyde.

Phase Separation and Bisulphite Adduct Formation

Centrifugation was used to separate the aqueous phase containing polymer-encapsulated cells from the organic phase. Methanol was added in amounts corresponding to the stock solution concentration to facilitate transfer between the aqueous and organic phases.

The organic phase was transferred to a separate flask, and aqueous sodium bisulphite was added followed by vigorous shaking. Sodium bisulphite selectively reacted with the aldehyde to form a stable, water-soluble adduct.

The adduct partitioned into the aqueous phase, leaving p-cymene in the organic layer. The two layers were separated using a separatory funnel, and the aqueous phase was collected.

Recovery of p-Cuminaldehyde

A 50% base (NaOH or Na2CO3) was added to the aqueous phase until the pH reached 12, releasing free p-cuminaldehyde.

Toluene was added, allowing the hydrophobic aldehyde to partition into the organic phase. The phases were separated using a separatory funnel, yielding an organic phase containing p-cuminaldehyde.

Drying and Solvent Removal

The organic phase was dried using anhydrous magnesium sulphate to remove residual water. The drying agent was removed by filtration.

Toluene was evaporated using a rotary evaporator (boiling point: 110 °C), leaving behind purified p-cuminaldehyde (Furigay et al., 2018).

Recovery and Reuse of Cells

The aqueous layer containing cells obtained after the initial centrifugation step underwent demulsification via further centrifugation and was subsequently resuspended in fresh media for continued growth.

During demulsification, residual polymer emulsion may adhere to the cell surface. This thin polymer layer can act as an additional diffusion barrier, explaining the relatively large decrease in activity observed during the first three cycles. Once the cell surface becomes uniformly coated, no further significant decrease in activity is observed in subsequent cycles.

Quantification

Reverse Phase High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (RP-HPLC)

HPLC Column Specifications

  • Column type: Cosmosil C18
  • Dimensions: 250 mm × 4.6 mm
  • Particle size: 5 µm

Preparation of Mobile Phase

Sodium sulphate, acetonitrile, and methanol were mixed in the ratio 20:73:7 (v/v).

The mobile phase was filtered through a 0.22 µm nylon membrane filter and degassed using a sonicator prior to use.

Preparation of Standard Stock Solution

A total of 10 mg of pure commercial p-cuminaldehyde was weighed accurately. The compound was first dissolved in 10 mL of acetonitrile, followed by dilution with 10 mL of methanol and shaken thoroughly to obtain a stock solution of 1000 µg/mL.

The stock solution was degassed using a sonicator and filtered through a 0.22 µm nylon membrane filter. Working standards of varying concentrations were prepared from this stock solution using the mobile phase as the diluent.

Preparation of Samples from Pseudomonas putida

100 µL of p-cuminaldehyde extracted from P. putida cultures was taken and diluted with 900 µL of the mobile phase. The dilution factor was adjusted depending on the yield obtained.

The sample was degassed using a sonicator and filtered through a 0.22 µm nylon membrane filter prior to injection.

Preparation of Standard Calibration Curve

Chromatographic analysis was performed using a reverse-phase Cosmosil C18 (4.6 × 250 mm, 5 µm) column. The column oven temperature and the HPLC system were maintained at 25 ± 2 °C.

A blank sample containing only the mobile phase was first injected to ensure baseline stability. Standard solutions of p-cuminaldehyde at different concentrations were then injected, and peak areas were recorded using a UV detector set at 326 nm to generate the calibration curve.

Experimental Run

  • Run a blank sample containing only the mobile phase.
  • Inject p-cuminaldehyde samples obtained from P. putida multiple times to ensure reproducibility.
  • Inject a standard sample at the end of the run to verify instrument stability and check for any signal drift.
