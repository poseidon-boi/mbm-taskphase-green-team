Whole-Genome BLAST Analysis Against the Target Aldehyde Gene

We performed a whole-genome BLAST analysis of Pseudomonas putida F1 against our target aldehyde-related gene (cymC) to identify homologous sequences and confirm the presence.

Before designing a gene knockout strategy, it was essential to:

  • Confirm that cymC is present as a single, well-defined locus in the genome
  • Check for paralogous or homologous genes that could compensate for cymC deletion
  • Ensure that targeting cymC would not unintentionally affect other aldehyde-metabolising enzymes
  • Verify that the gene sequence used for the Upstream Homologous Arm and the Downstream Homologous Arm design is accurate

A BLASTn search was performed using the Pseudomonas putida F1 genome as the subject.

Inference : BLAST analysis showed a perfect match between the query gene and the Pseudomonas putida F1 genome, with 100% identity and an E-value of 0, indicating a highly significant alignment.

Aldehyde Dehydrogenase Specificity Analysis

We performed a comparative BLAST analysis to evaluate whether other aldehyde dehydrogenase enzymes present in Pseudomonas putida F1 could potentially interfere with or compensate for the function of our target aldehyde-related gene (cymC).A. putida contains multiple aldehyde dehydrogenases involved in diverse metabolic pathways.
Before proceeding with a gene knockout strategy, it was important to assess:

  • Potential functional redundancy
  • Possible off-target aldehyde metabolism
  • The likelihood of other enzymes compensating for the loss of cymC

Reviewed aldehyde dehydrogenase proteins from P. putida F1 were retrieved from UniProt (Swiss-Prot). A total of six aldehyde dehydrogenase candidates were identified. The protein sequences were individually subjected to BLAST analysis with CymC.

The BLAST results show that while P. putida has other aldehyde dehydrogenases, the closest hits (betB and astD) have only moderate to low sequence similarity to cymC. This suggests they are unlikely to compensate for its function, confirming cymC as a specific and suitable knockout target.

Identification of Upstream and Downstream Homology Arms

We identified the regions flanking the target gene (cymC) in the P. putida F1 genome to design the upstream and downstream homology arms (UHA and DHA) required for homologous recombination.

Using the BLAST results from the genome vs. cymC gene, we determined the exact genomic coordinates of the target gene.

The orientation (plus/minus strand) was noted to correctly identify upstream and downstream regions.

Sequences upstream (UHA) and downstream (DHA) of the gene were extracted. Reverse complement sequences were converted to 5′ → 3′ orientation to prepare them for primer design and cloning.

Construction of the Insert DNA

UHA and DHA sequences were joined in silico to form a continuous segment.

Restriction sites for XmaI and PstI were added at the ends to allow the insert to ligate with the plasmid backbone.

Primers were designed specific to this segment with the following parameters:

  • Length: ~35 nucleotides
  • GC content: 50–60%
  • Melting temperature (Tm)

Future: Flux Balance Analysis (FBA)

Flux balance analysis (FBA) is a widely used approach for studying biochemical networks, particularly genome-scale metabolic network reconstructions that contain all known metabolic reactions in an organism and the genes that encode each enzyme (What Is Flux Balance Analysis?, n.d.).
It is a mathematical modelling technique to simulate biochemical reactions as a linear equation system in silico under the steady-state assumption. FBA allows us to analyse the pathways occurring in organisms, identify which reactions must be regulated for the end product, and suggest further optimisations concerning growth conditions and yield estimates.

Importance in our project

To enable rational metabolic engineering and reduce experimental trial-and-error, our project will incorporates Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) as a computational tool. FBA is a constraint-based modelling approach that predicts intracellular metabolic flux distributions under defined physiological constraints, without requiring enzyme kinetic parameters.
In our project, FBA will be used to evaluate the feasibility of redirecting carbon flux in Pseudomonas putida toward the production of p-cumic aldehyde from p-cymene, to understand the effect of the gene knockout, and to guide the selection of genetic interventions prior to wet-lab implementation.
A genome-scale metabolic model of Pseudomonas putida F1 will be used to simulate metabolic flux distributions under defined constraints. The engineered p-cymene to p-cumic aldehyde pathway will be integrated into the model, and FBA will be performed to evaluate pathway feasibility, identify metabolic bottlenecks, and predict trade-offs between growth and product formation.

GSMM

There is no published and well cited genome-scale metabolic model (GEM) of Pseudomonas putida F1 that can be used as the modelling framework. Hence, we will be using existing models developed for closely related strains (e.g., KT2440) and refine them to represent the metabolic capabilities specific to P. putida F1. The p-cymene catabolic pathway and engineered steps leading to p-cumic aldehyde formation and accumulation will then be incorporated into the model using stoichiometric information from literature and metabolic databases to get our final objectives.
The model assumes steady-state conditions, enforcing mass balance for all intracellular metabolites (S.v = 0), and applies biologically realistic constraints on reaction directionality and flux bounds.
The objective (knockout effect in our case) is written as:

Z=cTv

Where,

  • v = all reaction fluxes
  • c = a vector for the flux that has an effect on our objective
  • Z = the numerical score of the effectiveness of the solution

Proposed Protocol: Flux Balance Analysis

  • Step 1: Model Selection
    A curated genome-scale metabolic model of P. putida F1 will be selected (formulated if there is no published/well cited model for our strain) and validated for growth under minimal medium conditions.
  • Step 2: Pathway Integration
    Reactions constituting the p-cymene to p-cumic aldehyde pathway will be incorporated into the model with stoichiometric and thermodynamic constraints derived from literature and databases (KEGG, MetaCyc).
  • Step 3: Constraint Definition
    Physiological constraints such as substrate uptake rates, reaction reversibility, gene deletions, and reaction fluxes will be applied.
  • Step 4: Objective Formulation
    Multiple objective functions will be tested, including biomass maximization, aldehyde production maximization, and multi-objective (Pareto) optimization.
    FBA will be performed using biomass maximization and aldehyde production maximization.
  • Step 5: Decision-Making
    Flux distributions will be analyzed to identify bottlenecks and optimal engineering strategies.
  • Step 6: Experimental Translation
    FBA-supported strategies will guide gene knockouts, overexpression targets, and pathway balancing prior to wet-lab implementation.

Applications of FBA in Our Project

  1. Pathway Feasibility Assessment
    FBA is used to verify whether the engineered pathway can carry non-zero flux toward p-cumic aldehyde production under physiological constraints. This allows us to rule out infeasible pathway designs before experimental implementation.
  2. Identification of Metabolic Bottlenecks
    By analyzing predicted flux distributions and performing flux variability analysis (FVA), we identify reactions that limit carbon flow toward the target product. These reactions are prioritized as candidates for overexpression or pathway balancing.
  3. Evaluation of Gene Knockouts
    FBA simulations are used to assess the impact of knocking out competing reactions, such as aldehyde dehydrogenases that convert p-cumic aldehyde to downstream metabolites. This helps ensure that knockouts do not completely block growth or destabilize central metabolism (What Is Flux Balance Analysis?, n.d.).

Limitations

  • It cannot predict metabolite concentrations as it does not use kinetic parameters.
  • It can only determine the fluxes at steady state.
  • The reliability of this models predictions depends on the accuracy of the stoichiometric data.
