Human Practices

Survey

We conducted a survey to evaluate general awareness and public interest in our project. The objective was to understand how people perceive perfume production and to assess their response to perfumes produced via biosynthetic methods, particularly whether this influences their perception of the product or affects their purchasing decisions. This survey helped us gauge the potential societal impact of our project and within 24 hours, we received around 250 responses.


From our interactions, we found that a lot of people use fragrant products every day, while only a few use them rarely. This shows that fragrances are a regular part of daily life and not just an occasional luxury. Because these products are used so often, making their production more sustainable is sure to have a large positive impact over time. This further supported our decision to focus on developing environment friendly and scalable methods for producing fragrance compounds.


Out of the 313 responses, about 253 people (80.8%) reported using perfumes and deodorants. Soaps and shampoos were the next most commonly used fragrant products. This further stresses on the the importance of developing sustainable methods for producing fragrance compounds.


Most people (81.6%) know that fragrances are made chemically, which shows why we need greener, bio-based ways to make them.


Most people (81.6%) know fragrances are made chemically. When asked about making them with microbes, most said it didn’t change their view, 31% felt positive, and 9.9% felt negative. This shows microbial production doesn’t change perception much.


Most people preferred fresh, aquatic and floral fragrances while spicy scents seemed to be the least popular. Since cyclamen aldehyde has a floral and fresh character, our project aligns very well with consumer preferences. This therefore increases its relevance and acceptance.

Companies

We reached out to several companies that produce our target product to understand the market, including supply and demand dynamics. The main aim was to identify which industries use these compounds the most and to learn about the challenges companies face during production and the mechanism of its production process, so we can explore how our project could help address these issues. We are currently awaiting their responses.

We contacted perfumax India, a wholesaler/distributer company that gave us insights on the current trends and market for aromatic aldehydes in India. From the information they have given us, we have understood that in India, aldehydes normally do not go beyond 3000 rupees per kilogram in general, so in order for us to commercialise our product, our focus must be on making it economical. The supplier we spoke to also mentioned that the market for aldehydes has been depleting in the recent years, however, he was unaware of the replacements used for aromatic aldehydes when they were not available.

KK Aromas is a company that manufactures and deals aroma chemicals, including cumin aldehyde and cyclamen aldehyde. We reached out to this company in order to understand industry perspectives on the production methods and challenges faced with respect to cuminaldehyde and cyclamen aldehyde. In India most aldehydes are imported due to better quality and cleaner notes. He gave us valuable insights that helped us understand our impact better, and did suggest a more popular scent, d-limonene, for which unfortunately synbio production has already been done.
