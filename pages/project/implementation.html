Cyclamen Aldehyde

The popular Cyclamen or Lily of the Valley fragrance is derived from an aromatic compound known as cyclamen aldehyde, 3-(4-isopropylphenyl)-2-methylpropanal.

IUPAC name: 3-(4-isopropylphenyl)-2-methylpropanal

Scent profile: floral, fresh, rhubarb, musky, green

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Aldol condensation
  • Reactants (along with cuminaldehyde): propanal (excess)
  • Catalyst: 2 wt% Ru/C, sodium methoxide
  • Pressure: 10 MPa
  • Temperature: 25 °C

(Vrbková et al., 2015)

Figure 1: Aldol Condensation of Cuminaldehyde and Propanal to produce Cyclamen aldehyde (Vrbková et al., 2015)

Cuminyl Alcohol

This molecule is a component not only of cumin and caraway oils but also appears in eucalyptus, angelica, and bay leaf. It is a natural aromatic alcohol found in essential oils, fragrances, and flavours.

IUPAC name: 4-isopropylbenzyl alcohol

Scent profile: cumin, caraway, spicy, herbal, and leathery

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Cannizzaro reaction
  • Reactants (along with cuminaldehyde): N/A
  • Catalyst: NaOH

Figure 2: Cuminaldehyde to Cuminyl alcohol

Cinnamaldehyde

Cinnamaldehyde is a naturally occurring flavonoid that gives the spice cinnamon its flavour and odour. It occurs naturally in the bark of cinnamon trees and other species of the genus Cinnamomum, such as camphor and cassia.

IUPAC name: 3-phenylprop-2-enal

Scent profile: sweet, spicy, candy, cinnamon, warm

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Aldol condensation
  • Reactants (along with cuminaldehyde): acetaldehyde
  • Catalyst: 4-oxalochrotonate tautomerase (4-OT)

Figure 3: Synthesis of Cinnamaldehdye from p-Cuminaldehdye

Jasminaldehyde

It is a synthetic aroma chemical widely used in perfumery to provide floral notes, act as a fixative, and enhance stability in soaps, shampoos, and cosmetic formulations.

IUPAC name: 2-benzylideneheptanal

Scent profile: sweet, floral, fruity, herbaceous, honey-like, jasmine, green, metallic

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Aldol condensation
  • Reactants (along with cuminaldehyde): n-heptanal
  • Catalyst: NaOH, 0.15 M CTAB
  • Temperature: 60 °C

Figure 4: Reaction scheme for Jasminaldehyde synthesis (Velambath et al., 2022)

Benzoin

It is a natural resin obtained from the Styrax tree, often processed into a resinoid or absolute, where it provides richness and body to perfumes and traditional incense.

IUPAC name: 2-hydroxy-1,2-di(phenyl)ethanone

Scent profile: sweet, vanilla, balsamic, woody, powder

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Aldol condensation
  • Reactants (along with cuminaldehyde): potassium cyanide
  • Catalyst: N,N’-dimethylformamide

(Schowen et al., 1970)

Figure 5: Formation of Benzoine (Schowen et al., 1970)

Cinnamophenone

Closely related to the familiar cinnamon scent, this compound offers sweeter or balsamic interpretations that allow perfumers to craft unique fragrances. It is often blended with other aroma chemicals to develop a distinctive character.

IUPAC name: diphenylmethanone

Scent profile: floral, balsam, herbal

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Claisen–Schmidt condensation
  • Reactants (along with cuminaldehyde): acetophenone
  • Catalyst: aqueous NaOH, ethanol

(Kohler & Chadwell, 1922)

Figure 6: Synthesis of Cinnamophenone from Benzaldehyde (Kohler & Chadwell, 1922)

Methyl Cinnamate

A naturally occurring organic compound, a methyl ester of cinnamic acid, found commonly in strawberries, basil, and Sichuan pepper. It is investigated for its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties.

IUPAC name: methyl (2E)-3-phenylprop-2-enoate

Scent profile: strawberry, cherry, sweet, balsamic, cinnamyl.

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Condensation reaction
  • Reactants: Along with cuminaldehyde, acetic acid ester
  • Catalyst: Alcoholic solution of a metal alkoxide

(Okui et al., 1999)

Figure 7: Synthesis of Methyl Cinnamate from p-Cuminaldehyde.

Benzylidene Acetone

A naturally occurring organic compound related to cinnamic acid derivatives, found commonly in strawberries, basil, and Sichuan pepper. It is investigated for its potential anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties.

IUPAC name: (E)-4-phenylbut-3-en-2-one

Scent profile: rhubarb, creamy, floral, cinnamon, spicy, sweet, balsamic

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Claisen–Schmidt condensation
  • Reactants: Along with cuminaldehyde, acetone
  • Catalyst: NaOH

Figure 8: Bezylidene Acetone production (Sun et al., 2022)

Strawberry Glycidate 2

Ethyl phenylglycidate is an aromatic organic compound containing two functional groups: an ester and an epoxide. It is also known as “strawberry aldehyde,” although it is neither found in strawberries nor does it contain an aldehyde functional group.

IUPAC name: ethyl 3-phenyloxirane-2-carboxylate

Scent profile: sweet, strawberry, berry, floral, rose, honey

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Darzens reaction
  • Reactants: Along with cuminaldehyde, ethyl chloroacetate
  • Catalyst: metal alcoholate / sodium amide / metallic sodium under anhydrous conditions

Figure 9: Ethyl phenylglycidate formation (Kimura et al., 1983)

Cuminyl Acetate

It adds depth to perfumes, soaps, and detergents, often blending with oriental and spicy accords. It is valued for its stability and compatibility in personal care and cleaning products, providing a long-lasting spicy aroma.

IUPAC name: (4-propan-2-ylphenyl)methyl acetate

Scent profile: fresh, fruity, sweet, herbal, bergamot

Preparation:

  • Reaction: Fischer esterification
  • Reactants: Along with cuminaldehyde, ethanoic acid
  • Catalyst: HCl

(Chengwei et al., 2010)

Figure 10: Cuminyl Acetate formation through Fischer Esterification (Chengwei et al., 2010)
